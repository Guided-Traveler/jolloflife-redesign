A Beginner's Guide to Nigerian Spices & Seasonings

You have tasted the incredible flavors of Nigerian food and now you want to recreate them at home. The good news is that Nigerian cooking, while deeply flavorful, is not as complicated as it might seem -- it just requires the right spices. The challenge for many home cooks outside of Nigeria is knowing which spices to buy, where to find them, and how to use them. This guide will walk you through everything you need to build a Nigerian spice pantry from scratch, even if you are cooking from Winnipeg.

Where to Buy Nigerian Spices in Winnipeg and Across Canada

Winnipeg has a growing number of African and international grocery stores that carry the essential spices and ingredients for Nigerian cooking. Stores on and around Sargent Avenue, Ellice Avenue, and in the West End neighborhood stock a wide range of West African products, from dried crayfish and locust beans to palm oil and ground egusi. Look for shops that specifically advertise African or Nigerian groceries -- they will have the most authentic and freshest products.

If you cannot find a specific ingredient locally, online shopping has made it easier than ever to source Nigerian spices anywhere in Canada. Several online retailers specialize in West African ingredients and ship across the country. Amazon Canada also carries many Nigerian spices, though specialty shops tend to offer better quality and fresher stock. When buying dried spices online, check the packaging date and look for whole spices rather than pre-ground whenever possible, as they retain their flavor much longer.

Your Essential Nigerian Spice Pantry

If you are starting from zero, here is the list of spices and seasonings you should acquire first. With these in your pantry, you will be able to make the vast majority of Nigerian dishes:

  • Dried crayfish (ground) -- The single most important seasoning in Nigerian cooking. Used in almost every soup and stew. Buy it whole and grind as needed for the freshest flavor.
  • Scotch bonnet peppers -- Fresh or frozen, these bring the heat. If you cannot find scotch bonnets, habanero peppers are a close substitute.
  • Locust beans (iru/dawadawa) -- Fermented and intensely savory. Buy the fresh (moist) variety if available, or the dried version which keeps longer.
  • Dried thyme -- Used much more liberally in Nigerian cooking than in European cuisine. A staple in jollof rice, fried rice, and many stews.
  • Curry powder -- Nigerian curry powder is typically a mild, yellow blend used in fried rice and some stews. It adds color and a gentle warmth.
  • Onion powder and garlic powder -- Convenient additions to many dishes, though fresh onions and garlic are always preferred when available.
  • Ground cameroon pepper (dried scotch bonnet) -- A powder made from sun-dried scotch bonnet peppers. It adds heat without the moisture of fresh peppers.
  • Ehuru (calabash nutmeg) -- A warm, aromatic spice. Buy whole and grate as needed. Used in egusi soup and pepper soup.
  • Uziza seeds -- Peppery and slightly bitter. Ground into pepper soup and some traditional sauces.
  • Palm oil (red palm oil) -- Technically not a spice, but it is essential. It provides the distinctive red-orange color and rich flavor to many Nigerian dishes.
  • Bouillon cubes or powder (Maggi/Knorr) -- Widely used in Nigerian cooking as a seasoning base. Chicken or beef varieties are most common.

How to Store Nigerian Spices for Maximum Freshness

Proper storage is the difference between spices that sing and spices that sit flat. Dried crayfish, once ground, should be stored in an airtight container in the refrigerator or freezer, where it will keep its potency for several months. Whole dried crayfish can be kept in a cool, dry cupboard for up to six months. Locust beans, particularly the fresh variety, should be stored in the freezer in a sealed container -- they freeze beautifully and can be used directly from frozen.

Scotch bonnet peppers can be frozen whole or blended into a paste and stored in ice cube trays for convenient portioning. Ground spices like ehuru, uziza, and cameroon pepper should be kept in airtight glass jars away from heat and light. As a general rule, ground spices lose their potency within six months, so buy in small quantities and replace regularly rather than stocking up. Whole spices last much longer, which is why buying whole and grinding fresh is always the better approach when possible. Palm oil keeps well at room temperature in a dark bottle for several months.

Basic Spice Combinations for Common Dishes

One of the beautiful things about Nigerian cooking is that the spice combinations, once you learn them, become intuitive. Here are the core formulas for some of the most popular dishes:

Jollof Rice: The base flavor comes from blended tomatoes, scotch bonnet peppers, and onions. Season with dried thyme, curry powder, bay leaves, bouillon cubes, and a touch of ground crayfish. The secret to party-style jollof is a slightly smoky finish -- let the bottom of the pot toast just a little before serving.

Egusi Soup: Start with palm oil, onions, and your pepper base. Add ground egusi (melon seeds), then season with dried crayfish, locust beans, ehuru (grated), and bouillon. Finish with fresh leafy greens (spinach or bitter leaf) and assorted meats. Each spice layer adds depth.

Pepper Soup: This is where the aromatic spices shine. Combine uziza seeds, ehuru, alligator pepper (or substitute black pepper), dried ginger, and scotch bonnet pepper with your choice of protein simmered in a light broth. Some cooks add scent leaves at the very end for fragrance. The spice blend can be prepared in advance and stored for convenience.

Tomato Stew (base for rice and yam): Blend tomatoes, scotch bonnet peppers, and onions, then fry in palm oil or vegetable oil until the raw tomato taste cooks out and the oil floats to the top. Season with thyme, curry powder, bouillon, and dried crayfish. This base stew is one of the most versatile preparations in Nigerian cooking.

Tips for Cooking with Nigerian Spices at Home

The most important tip for anyone new to Nigerian cooking is to taste as you go. Nigerian recipes are guidelines, not rigid formulas -- every cook adjusts the heat, salt, and seasoning to their own preference. Start with less scotch bonnet pepper than the recipe calls for and add more gradually until you reach your desired heat level. Remember that the heat intensifies as the dish cooks, so what tastes mildly spicy early on may become quite hot by the time it is finished.

When using dried crayfish, remember that a little goes a long way. Its flavor is concentrated, and too much can overpower a dish. Start with a tablespoon for a pot that serves four to six people and adjust from there. With locust beans, the aroma is much stronger than the flavor -- do not let the smell put you off. Once it dissolves into the cooking liquid, it adds a savory depth that is subtle and deeply satisfying.

Finally, do not be afraid to experiment. Nigerian home cooks rarely follow exact measurements. They cook by feel, taste, and experience. The more you cook with these spices, the more familiar their flavors will become, and the more confident you will feel adjusting them to your liking.
