Dietary Accommodations & Allergies in Nigerian Food

One of the most common questions we hear at Jollof Life is whether Nigerian food can accommodate specific dietary needs. The answer might surprise you. Nigerian cuisine, rooted in centuries of cooking with whole grains, legumes, tubers, and fresh vegetables, is naturally more inclusive than many people realize. While some dishes do contain common allergens, the diversity of West African cooking means there are almost always delicious alternatives available. Here is everything you need to know about navigating Nigerian food with dietary restrictions or allergies.

Naturally Gluten-Free: A Cuisine Built on Rice, Yam, and Cassava

If you follow a gluten-free diet, Nigerian cuisine is one of the most accommodating you will find anywhere. The staple carbohydrates in Nigerian cooking are almost all naturally free of gluten. Rice -- whether it is jollof rice, fried rice, ofada rice, or coconut rice -- is a cornerstone of the cuisine and is entirely gluten-free. Yam, served pounded, boiled, fried, or as amala (yam flour), contains no gluten. Cassava, processed into garri, fufu, or eba, is another gluten-free staple that appears on nearly every Nigerian table.

Plantain, both fried (dodo) and boiled, is also gluten-free and features heavily across the menu. The same goes for beans, which form the base of dishes like moi moi (steamed bean pudding) and akara (bean fritters). In fact, you could eat an entire multi-course Nigerian meal -- from starters through mains to dessert -- without encountering a single grain of wheat. The main items to watch for are puff puff (fried dough balls made with wheat flour), meat pies, and some chin chin varieties, which do contain wheat.

Vegan and Vegetarian Options in Nigerian Cooking

Nigerian cuisine has a rich tradition of plant-based cooking that existed long before veganism became a global trend. Many dishes are either naturally vegan or can be easily adapted. Beans porridge, made with black-eyed peas cooked in a peppery tomato sauce with palm oil, is a hearty vegan dish that is a staple across Nigeria. Fried plantain (dodo) is entirely plant-based. Jollof rice, when prepared without stock, is vegan-friendly, as are many vegetable soups when made with mushroom or vegetable stock instead of meat or fish stock.

For vegetarians, the options expand even further. Moi moi can be made without eggs and still holds its shape beautifully. Efo riro (spinach stew) and other vegetable soups can be prepared with plant-based proteins and palm oil. Akara -- crispy bean fritters -- is naturally vegan when made in the traditional way with just beans, peppers, and onions. At Jollof Life, we understand that dietary choices are personal and important. We are always happy to discuss which dishes on our menu are suitable for vegan or vegetarian diners, and our kitchen can adjust many dishes on request.

Common Allergens to Be Aware Of

While Nigerian food is naturally accommodating in many ways, there are some common allergens that diners should be aware of. Peanuts (groundnuts) are widely used in Nigerian cooking -- most notably in suya spice (yaji), groundnut soup, and as a thickening agent in certain stews. If you have a peanut allergy, it is essential to ask about suya and any dish described as having a groundnut base.

Shellfish and fish products are another common presence. Dried crayfish (ground into powder) is used as a seasoning in a vast number of soups and stews, including egusi, ogbono, and many tomato-based sauces. Stockfish (dried cod) and dried fish also appear frequently. If you have a shellfish or fish allergy, these are the ingredients to flag when ordering. Palm oil, while not a common allergen, is used extensively and gives many Nigerian dishes their distinctive red-orange color. It is worth knowing about if you have specific oil sensitivities.

How Jollof Life Accommodates Your Needs

At Jollof Life, we believe everyone deserves to enjoy the incredible flavors of Nigerian cuisine. Our staff is trained to discuss ingredients and help you navigate our menu based on your dietary needs. For catering events, we can create entirely customized menus that account for allergies, religious dietary laws (including halal requirements), and lifestyle choices like veganism or keto diets.

We encourage guests to let us know about any allergies or dietary restrictions when ordering, whether you are dining in, ordering online, or planning an event through our catering service. Nigerian food is inherently diverse and flexible -- with over 100 dishes on our menu, there is always something delicious waiting for you, no matter your dietary requirements.
